It's very important that scientists not work in a vacuum, but actively engage with the public. I have been involved in a number of public engagement projects of all kinds; running workshops, working with students, giving public talks, and all sorts of other stuff.

I am the founder and director of an education project called Student2Scientist. This project aimed introduce computer programming centrally in the STEM curriculum, and demonstrate to students the importance of computing in modern scientific research. In 2013 we were funded by Google, and worked specifically with girls' schools with the aim of helping to redress the gender-imbalance in the sciences. In 2014, funded by SFI and working in collaboration with students, teachers, and academics nationwide, we developed a new course for the Junior Cycle called "Space Science and Coding."

A few years ago I set up a small business in Dublin, Codify, running programming workshops aimed at adults. I've left it in the hands of a very lovely and enthusiastic bunch of people, if you're interested in learning to code why not head along!

Sunspotters is a really cool citizen science project that I have worked on where members of the public can become involved in active research. It's really hard to decide algorithmically whether or not a sunspot is more or less complex than another, but by having volunteers categorise sunspots we can get a large, unique data set that allows researchers to test the idea that more complex sunspots lead to bigger solar flares.

A few years ago I set up the group Love Irish Science to provide a lobbying voice to Irish scientists working on fundamental research.

I do comet-making workshops for secondary school kids. You can download the slides of a talk (and my secret comet recipe) here.

I also do a little make-your-own interferometer workshop. Why not try it yourself? Why not indeed.
