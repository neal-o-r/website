I studied Theoretical Physics at TCD for my undergrad degree, before specialising in Astrophysics and going on to pursue a PhD.

Generally speaking, my research concerned the atmospheres of old, cool stars, and how light travels through them. As you can imagine that's a fairly big subject, and within it I did a broad range of research.

One of my primary interests was radiative transfer modelling. This involves making semi-emprical models of a star's atmosphere, and determining, as a function of wavelength, how photons travel through the atmosphere and interact with the plasma. These kinds of models provide an interpretational framework for observations, and give new insights into the kinds of physics going on in stellar atmospheres.

A lot of my recent research concerned ζ Aurigae binaries. These systems consist of a very large, cool star orbited by a small, very hot star. When the larger eclipses the smaller, the hot star acts as a light source shining through the atmosphere of the cooler. This is a fairly rare configuration, and by careful observation and modelling we can learn things about stellar atmospheres that would be impossible to find out any other way.

Another of my research interests was interferometry, primarily at optical and IR wavelengths. Spectro-interferometry at these wavelengths can be used to study the extent of chromospheric line-forming regions and to measure the star's intensity distribution. In the summer of 2013 I was awarded the Fizeau Optical Interferometry Award and spent 2 months working at the Observatoire De La Côte D'Azur, collaborating with the group there on the interpretation of CHARA observations.

I have also been involved in an attempt to observe the a hot Jupiter orbiting Pollux. We used the GMRT and attempted to observe the electron-cyclotron maser emission from the aurora of the planet, caused by the interaction of the star's wind and the planet.

If you want the excruciating detail, you can read my thesis. Enjoy!
